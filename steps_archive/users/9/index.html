Introduction

Since becoming a private math tutor, I've been able to get a lot of insights as to why a lot of students struggled with math. There are many smaller reasons, but they large fall into two categories: being unable to actually understand the material, and lack of guided practice.

When students first learn math in elementary school, they start with arithmetic. It is largely intuitive, and for those who don't really "get it," a teacher can bring some sense to them by demonstrating visually. If a student doesn't really understand why 47 + 29 equals 76, the teacher can perhaps lay out 4 dimes and 7 pennies for the first number, 2 dimes and 9 pennies for the second one, and show that their combination results in 6 dimes and 16 pennies, which is 76 cents.

This visual approach, however, quickly becomes difficult as the numbers get bigger, or as students start to learn fractions and decimal. A teacher can only come up with so many 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, ... , 1/24 pieces of shapes to teach their students how to add and subtract fractions. As a result, teachers end up teaching a set of rules and steps to take in order to arrive at an answer, rather than teaching to arrive at a conclusion by taking a set of logical next steps.

Consequently, for those who don't "get" math, math becomes a set of arbitrary rules and instructions that have no bearing in real life. To them, math (and later on, science) is nothing more than a hoax, sort of like alchemy and astrology.

To remedy the problem, I have been making a set of apps that can visually and logically demonstrate why the math principles they illustrate are true. Then I've began to organize those apps onto a platform (still a work in progress) in order to systematically record and observe students performance.

Another big problem that hinders the effort of the students is the lack of motivation. To compare, almost everyone knows the benefit of eating vegetable and exercising, whether they like it or not; almost everyone knows the benefit of practicing drawing, as it could lead to stunning drawing later. Most students, however, don't see any benefit of doing math. For the vast majority, especially those who are struggling, the biggest question in math is "why are we evening learning this?" The simple truth is that the vast majority of them will not end up using math in their future careers. That's why I've implemented a reward system that motivates students (especially younger ones) to earn coins in order to watch videos.

Area of Triangle From Coordinates

This exercise trains students to find the area of a triangle formed from three random points on a coordinate plane.

Although there's a forumla produces the area, without the knowledge of the formula, student must exhibit a range of abilities in order to solve the problem.

First, perhaps most obviously, the student must know what a coordinate plane is and how the coordinate system works.

Secondly, the student must be able find distance between two points.

Lastly, the student must know how to find an equation between two points on a coordinate plane and then be able to find a point on the line with a given x or y value.

Counting

Built as a tool to help kids visually understand numbers beyond 100 and subsequently adding them, this exercise lets the user to practice recognizing the number of blocks by counting the set of blocks in different units that are represented in different colors.

Division Inutition 2

Traditionally, when a student learns division, he is told to follow a certain set of steps in order to arrive at the answer. Students are almost never taught why those steps work

The app is designed to let students do divisions by hand in order to develop a sense of how it all works.

Division Inutition 3

The premise behind the development is same as Division Intuition 2, but for divisors that are greater than 10.

Also unlikely Division intuition 2, it employs a story problem to logically derive the solution.

Finding Factors

A large chunk of elementary school math can become absolutely trivial if the student knows how to find all the factors of a number, and know how to work with them.

This exercise trains students to find the number of factors, then all the factors of a given number.

Number Pattern

In this exercise, the given set of numbers have a constant difference. The student's job is to recognie the pattern and find the next number.

Permutation and Combination

This app lets students try out what all the permutation and combination of a certain set looks like.

Because finding the permutation requires factorial operation, the number of elements that can be permutated is limited to 7.

Should you decide to permutate a set of 7 characters, you might have to wait for a bit until the operation finishes.

Finding a Parabola Through Three Points

This exercise lets the student practice finding the parabola that fits through a set of three random points on a cartesian coordinate plane. It requires the knowledge of parabola, and matrix operation called row reduction.
